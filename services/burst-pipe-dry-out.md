Burst pipes and plumbing leaks can send clean water through walls, ceilings, flooring, cabinets, and basement finishes quickly. Even after the plumber stops the leak, wet materials can remain behind drywall, trim, insulation, carpet pad, subflooring, and cabinets.

Dry-out work focuses on finding the affected areas, removing water, deciding what can be dried, and documenting the condition before repairs begin. The sooner drying starts, the easier it is to limit secondary damage.

## What this service may include

- Safety check around wet electrical areas
- Water shutoff guidance if the source is still active and reachable
- Standing water extraction
- Moisture checks around walls, ceilings, floors, cabinets, and trim
- Drying equipment recommendations
- Removal recommendations for saturated materials
- Photos and drying documentation

## When to call

Call or text after a frozen pipe, broken supply line, water heater leak, sink overflow, appliance connection failure, or hidden leak that has wet walls, floors, ceilings, or cabinets. If water is near electrical systems, stay clear and call for emergency help.

## Local St. Louis considerations

Winter cold snaps can freeze pipes in exterior walls, crawlspaces, garages, and poorly insulated areas. Older homes may also have plumbing routes that make leaks harder to trace. Drying should account for plaster, drywall, trim, subflooring, basement finishes, and humidity.

## After the leak is stopped

Take photos before cleanup, keep removed damaged materials for documentation when practical, and ask what areas were checked for hidden moisture. A dry-looking ceiling or baseboard can still hide moisture after a pipe leak.


## Why hidden moisture is common after pipe leaks

A plumbing leak often runs along framing, under flooring, through ceiling cavities, and behind trim before it becomes obvious. The visible puddle may be only part of the loss. A burst pipe above a finished ceiling can wet insulation and drywall. A sink or supply-line leak can soak cabinet bases and the flooring underneath. A basement pipe can wet studs, baseboards, and stored contents.

Dry-out planning should identify the wet boundary, not just the place where water was first noticed. That may include moisture checks in adjacent rooms, below the leak, behind baseboards, and around cabinets or built-ins.

## Helpful details to share when you call

Share whether the water is turned off, where the pipe or fixture is located, how long water ran, what rooms are wet, and whether ceilings, walls, cabinets, carpet, or flooring are affected. If a plumber has already repaired the leak, drying can still be urgent.
