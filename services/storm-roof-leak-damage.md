Storm and roof leak water damage can start in the attic, ceiling, wall cavity, basement, or around windows before it reaches the room you can see. Wind-driven rain, hail damage, missing shingles, flashing failures, sump pump overflow, and flash flooding can all leave hidden moisture behind finished materials.

The first step is to control the source when possible and protect the structure from additional water. After that, drying and documentation help determine what can be saved and what needs repair.

## What this service may include

- Safety check around wet ceilings, outlets, and standing water
- Photos and affected-area documentation
- Water extraction when floors or basements are wet
- Moisture checks around ceilings, walls, trim, and flooring
- Drying equipment recommendations
- Temporary protection planning when additional rain is expected
- Follow-up notes for roofing, plumbing, repair, or insurance conversations

## When to call

Call or text when water is dripping from a ceiling, stains are spreading, flooring is wet after a storm, a sump pump failed, water entered through foundation walls, or wind-driven rain has affected walls, windows, or attic areas.

## Local St. Louis considerations

The St. Louis metro sees heavy rain, hail, wind, freeze-thaw cycles, and flash-flooding events. Older roofs, finished basements, mature trees, clogged gutters, and sump pump dependence can all make storm-related water damage more complicated.

## Before repairs begin

Take photos, protect safe areas from additional water, avoid rooms with sagging ceilings, and do not assume the damage is limited to the visible stain. Moisture can travel along framing, insulation, and wall cavities.


## Why storm leaks can spread beyond the stain

A ceiling stain may be the first visible sign, but storm water can travel along rafters, insulation, wall framing, window trim, and flooring before it drips into the room. If the roof or exterior opening is still exposed, the property may need temporary protection before drying can succeed.

Storm damage work often involves more than one trade. Emergency restoration can address water extraction, drying, affected materials, and documentation, while roofing, gutter, exterior, or plumbing repairs may be needed to stop the source permanently. Clear photos and notes help connect those steps.

## Helpful details to share when you call

Share when the leak started, whether rain is still coming in, whether the ceiling is sagging, what rooms are affected, whether attic access is available, and whether water reached floors, walls, or electrical fixtures. Avoid rooms with sagging ceilings or active electrical hazards.
