Flooded basement cleanup in St. Louis often involves more than pumping out visible water. Finished basements, older foundations, clay soil, failed sump pumps, sewer backups, and heavy storms can leave moisture behind drywall, trim, carpet pad, cabinets, stored contents, and framing.

The first priorities are safety, source control, water removal, drying, and documentation. If electricity may be involved, sewage may be present, or water is still rising, stay out of the area until it is safe.

## What this service may include

- Safety check before entering the basement
- Water source review: sump pump, stormwater, seepage, drain backup, pipe leak, or appliance overflow
- Standing water extraction
- Wet contents and storage-box guidance
- Carpet, pad, trim, and drywall recommendations
- Dehumidification and air movement
- Moisture checks and drying documentation

## When to call

Call or text when you see standing water, wet drywall, soaked carpet pad, sagging ceilings, musty odor after a leak, sewage, or water that may have reached electrical systems. If there is immediate danger, call local emergency services first.

## Local St. Louis considerations

Many St. Louis properties have basements, older plumbing, clay sewer laterals, heavy spring storms, and winter freeze risks. A local restoration response should account for building age, basement construction, humidity, foundation seepage, sump pump history, and insurance documentation needs.

## What to do before help arrives

If it is safe, stop the source, move valuables away from wet areas, avoid walking through contaminated water, and take photos before cleanup begins. Do not plug in fans or equipment in wet areas if outlets or cords may be affected.


## Why basement flooding needs careful drying

Basements can hold moisture in places that are easy to miss: carpet pad, baseboards, wall cavities, storage shelving, boxed contents, stair trim, insulation, and the lower edges of finished walls. If the cleanup only removes visible water, humidity can stay elevated and wet materials can keep releasing moisture into the room.

A good cleanup plan separates the source of the water from the drying problem. Clean supply-line water, stormwater, seepage, and sewage backups may require different safety steps and different material decisions. The scope should be based on where the water traveled and what materials were affected.

## Helpful details to share when you call

Share the water depth, whether the sump pump is working, whether water came through a drain or foundation wall, whether carpet or drywall is wet, and whether there is any sewage odor. If the water is still rising or electrical systems may be affected, stay out of the basement.
