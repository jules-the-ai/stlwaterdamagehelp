A sewage backup is not a normal wet-vac cleanup. Water from sewer lines, drain backups, toilet overflows, and contaminated stormwater can contain bacteria, viruses, and other hazards. Keep children, pets, and unprotected adults out of the area and avoid touching affected materials.

Sewage cleanup usually requires containment, removal of contaminated porous materials, cleaning, disinfection, odor control, drying, and documentation. Blowing air across contaminated water or trying to save materials that should be removed can spread the problem.

## What this service may include

- Safety-first site review
- Source and affected-area documentation
- Containment recommendations
- Removal guidance for contaminated porous materials
- Cleaning and disinfection planning
- Odor control and drying support
- Photos and notes for repair or insurance conversations

## When to call

Call or text when sewage is visible, a floor drain has backed up, a toilet overflow involved waste, water has reached carpet or drywall, or a basement smells strongly after a drain backup. For municipal sewer concerns, you may also need to contact the sewer district or utility.

## Local St. Louis considerations

Some St. Louis properties have older sewer laterals, basement floor drains, combined storm events, and low-level fixtures that can be affected by backups. Fast action matters because contaminated water can affect flooring, trim, drywall, stored contents, and HVAC-adjacent areas.

## What not to do

Do not let children or pets into the area. Do not use household fans before contaminated materials are handled. Do not assume carpet, pad, cardboard, or upholstered contents can be safely dried after sewage exposure.


## Why sewage cleanup is different

Sewage and drain backups are treated differently from a clean supply-line leak because the water may be contaminated. Porous materials such as carpet pad, cardboard, upholstery, insulation, and some drywall may not be safe to simply dry in place. Cleanup should focus on reducing exposure, removing unsalvageable materials, cleaning affected hard surfaces, disinfecting where appropriate, controlling odor, and drying the structure.

Documentation also matters. Photos, affected-room notes, removed-material notes, and drying records can help homeowners, landlords, property managers, plumbers, sewer utilities, and insurance carriers understand what happened and what work followed.

## Helpful details to share when you call

Share which drain or fixture backed up, whether sewage is visible, which rooms are affected, whether HVAC equipment or stored belongings are nearby, and whether anyone has already entered the area. Keep traffic through the affected area as low as possible.
