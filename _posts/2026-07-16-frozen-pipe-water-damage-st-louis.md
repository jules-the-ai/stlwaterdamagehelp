Frozen pipes are one of the sneakiest winter water damage problems in St. Louis. A pipe can freeze during a cold snap, split under pressure, and then release water when the line thaws. If you are dealing with **frozen pipe water damage in St. Louis**, the first goal is simple: stop the water safely, document the damage, and get wet materials drying before hidden moisture spreads.

## Why frozen pipes cause water damage after the thaw

The City of St. Louis has warned that water expands when it freezes and that pipes exposed to temperatures below 20 degrees for extended periods are at greatest risk, with major damage often showing up when pipes thaw: [City extreme cold preparation guidance](https://www.stlouis-mo.gov/government/departments/public-safety/emergency-management/news/city-preparations-extreme-cold-jan-21-2026.cfm).

That means a home can seem fine during the coldest hours, then suddenly have water running into a ceiling, wall cavity, basement, cabinet, or finished room later. Common trouble spots include:

- pipes on exterior walls
- crawl spaces and unheated basements
- garages
- sink cabinets along outside walls
- hose bibs and outdoor spigots
- vacant homes or rooms with the heat turned down
- finished basement ceilings hiding plumbing lines

If water is actively running, treat it as an emergency. A few minutes can soak flooring, drywall, trim, insulation, cabinets, and belongings.

## First: shut off water if you can do it safely

If you know where the shut-off valve is and can reach it without stepping into unsafe water, turn off the water supply. The City recommends knowing the location of your shut-off valve before an emergency and says residents can request an emergency water shut-off at the tee head by calling 314-771-4880: [City extreme cold preparation guidance](https://www.stlouis-mo.gov/government/departments/public-safety/emergency-management/news/city-preparations-extreme-cold-jan-21-2026.cfm).

If you see leaking water outside, a possible water main issue, or a public water problem, the City also directs residents to report water problems through the Citizens' Service Bureau or the Water Division emergency line: [report a City water problem](https://www.stlouis-mo.gov/government/departments/public-utilities/water/report-water-problems.cfm).

Do not step into standing water if electrical outlets, cords, appliances, the breaker panel, or furnace equipment may be affected. If you are unsure, wait for qualified help.

## Then document the damage before cleanup moves too far

Before soaked materials are moved, take photos and short videos. Capture:

- the room from several angles
- the suspected pipe or water source
- wet ceilings, walls, baseboards, flooring, cabinets, and contents
- visible water lines or staining
- any standing water
- damaged personal property
- equipment placed for extraction or drying

Documentation helps you explain the loss to a plumber, restoration company, landlord, property manager, or insurance adjuster. It also creates a record if more hidden moisture is discovered later.

## Remove standing water and start structural drying

After a frozen or burst pipe, the visible puddle is only part of the problem. Water can travel under flooring, behind baseboards, down wall cavities, through insulation, and into basement ceilings.

A restoration crew should be able to help with:

- emergency water extraction
- removal of saturated carpet pad or unsalvageable porous materials
- moisture readings in walls, floors, ceilings, and trim
- dehumidifiers and controlled airflow
- daily drying checks when needed
- written notes and photos for your records

## Be careful with cabinets, walls, and finished basements

Frozen pipe leaks often start inside a wall or cabinet. Even if the floor looks dry later, moisture may still be trapped in:

- cabinet toe kicks
- drywall behind vanities or kitchen cabinets
- wall insulation
- baseboards and trim
- laminate or engineered flooring
- carpet pad
- basement ceiling cavities

That is why “put a fan on it” is not always enough. Airflow can help, but drying decisions should be based on what is wet, how wet it is, and whether the material can realistically dry in place.

The EPA advises fixing plumbing leaks and other water problems as soon as possible and drying items completely. The EPA also notes that absorbent or porous materials such as ceiling tiles and carpet may have to be thrown away if they become moldy because mold can grow into porous spaces: [EPA mold cleanup guidance](https://www.epa.gov/mold/mold-cleanup-your-home).

## What to ask a restoration company after a burst pipe

When you call for frozen pipe water damage help, ask practical questions:

1. Can you respond today for extraction and drying?
2. Do you handle hidden moisture checks behind walls and under flooring?
3. Will you document moisture readings and affected materials?
4. What materials can be dried in place, and what may need removal?
5. Can you coordinate with a plumber if the broken pipe still needs repair?
6. Do you work with homeowners, tenants, landlords, and property managers?
7. Will you separate emergency mitigation from later repairs on the invoice?

A good answer should be specific. You want a plan for stopping additional damage, not just a promise that everything will be fine.

## How to reduce frozen pipe risk next time

Once the emergency is under control, look at prevention. The City of St. Louis recommends steps such as letting cold water drip from faucets on exterior walls, opening cabinet doors under sinks, insulating pipes on exterior walls, disconnecting hoses from outdoor spigots, covering spigots with foam insulators, and never using an open flame to warm pipes: [City extreme cold preparation guidance](https://www.stlouis-mo.gov/government/departments/public-safety/emergency-management/news/city-preparations-extreme-cold-jan-21-2026.cfm).

If you travel during winter, consider shutting off the water supply, opening faucets, and keeping enough heat on to protect vulnerable areas. For rental properties and vacant homes, regular winter checks can prevent a small freeze event from becoming a multi-room water loss.

Call or text for help right away if water is actively running, a ceiling is sagging, electrical systems may be affected, carpet or drywall is soaked, or you suspect water traveled into a wall or finished basement cavity.
